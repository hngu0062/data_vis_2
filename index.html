The Cultural Demographics of Australia

An examination of the language usage and birthplaces of Australians in recent history
by Faustina Nguyen

Australia is a rich and vibrant country comprising many ethnic groups within its population.

The overwhelming majority of Australians speak English (almost 93% as of 2016), which is the official national language of Australia. Only a few other countries around the world match that statistic, most notably, the United Kingdom, New Zealand and the United States of America, which are all considered to be Western and developed like Australia.

So where is this acclaimed diversity in Australia?

At first glance, it may not seem that a country with such a dominant language would have much cultural variety.

However, census data from within the past two decades shows that this is, indeed, the case.

It appears that the bigger city areas of Australia tend to have the highest proportion known of multilinguals, with both New South Wales and Victoria having almost 30% of their respective populations claiming to be able to speak another language.

The same languages make up the top 6 in both states as well as for the umbrella category of greater capital cities, but in different orders.
These are:

  • Mandarin
  • Cantonese
  • Arabic
  • Vietnamese
  • Italian
  • Greek

The Northern Territory sports an even larger fraction of multilingual people, if only by a sliver.

Unlike the other states, however, the most commonly spoken LOTE there are Australian Indigenous languages, by a landslide.

One thing to bear in mind in the face of this information is that the NT is not densely populated, only having about 225,000 people, so the cultural demographic of the NT is not as representative of the wider Australian demographic as the aforementioned big cities, which have individual populations in excess of 5 million.

Across all available times and areas, the number of multilingual people who are sufficiently proficient in English dwarfs the number of those who are not. This would explain why Australia has such a high percentage of English-speakers while accommodating a wide range of backgrounds and cultures.

In recent years, there has been a boom in the prevalence of East and Southeast Asian languages across the board, especially Mandarin Chinese, rivalling or even overtaking the established Italian and Greek populations. Again, this is more of a characteristic of the bigger cities.

Unsurprisingly, the areas with relatively high proportions of Australian, English, Irish or Scottish ancestry also have the highest proportion of people who only speak English. Tasmania, Queensland and Regional Australia stand out in this regard.

Another interesting and perhaps unexpected observation to make here is the very recent overtaking of Australian ancestry by those with an English background. On the other hand, we can see that both populations have been neck-in-neck at the top of the list for at least decades, so this almost nation-wide occurrence may simply be a coincidence.


By using the slider to vary time, it is clear that Australia's population has not only been growing in absolute value, but also in cultural diversity, as the proportion of monolingual English-speakers to multilingual people has been diminishing.

The next question to ask is, "Is this is reflected in migration data?"

Birthplaces of Australians

source: .id demographic resources


The answer is a resounding "yes".

As could be expected, the most common overseas birthplace is the United Kingdom, consistent with our observation from the ancenstry census data. New Zealand also ranks quite highly in all. This would account for a large chunk of the top non-Australian, predominantly English-speaking ancestries.

Once again, we see the East and Southeast Asian identities catching up to the established European communities, if not outright overtaking them in number.


We also see that the percentage of Australians born overseas has been increasing over time in all areas since 2001. Tasmania and Regional Australia, two of the most remote areas in consideration, which were both shown to have relatively high monolingual English-speaking populations, are lagging behind the rest in this development.

Overall, the proportion of Australians who were born overseas in 2016 (26.3%) has increased by about 4.5% since 2001 (21.8%)



Australia continues to become more and more culturally diverse, a home for any range of future generations to come.
